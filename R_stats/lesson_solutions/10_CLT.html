Central Limit Theorem

As noted in the previous lesson, we cannot generate sampling distributions in practical situations. So how do we access the necessary aggregate behavior of sampled statistics and use it to our benefit?

The Central Limit Theorem, at its core, simply shows that many common sampling distributions can be modeled well by a normal distribution. And dont worry, it provides formulas for the parameters too!

Here are two important translations of the Central Limit Theorem that will be applicable to us in the near future.

CLT for proportions

Lets assume the actual population percentage is \(p\), and we consider samples of size \(n\).

  • Assumptions: If we have \(np > 10\) and \(n(1-p)>10\), the CLT applies reasonably well.
  • Result: The sampling distribution of sample percentages (\(\hat p\)) can be modeled with a normal distribution where
    • the mean is \(p\), and
    • the standard deviation is \(\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\).

Example. Suppose there is a jar of Jelly beans, and we have to guess the percentage of green jelly beans in the jar. We can access a random sample of 200 jelly beans.

  • I pre-loaded a simulated sampling distribution of percents, samp_dist_pct, based on samples of size 200.
  • The actual percentage of green in the entire jar was 0.09146.

Lets compare the sampling distribution against the CLT predictions!

# parameters
mu <- 0.09146

sigma <- sqrt( 0.09146*(1-0.09146)/200 )

#On your own, try out a probability versus percentage comparison
# What percentage of the sample percents were below 8.5%?
sum(samp_dist_pct < 0.085)/length(samp_dist_pct)
## [1] 0.3458
# Using the CLT and the appropriate normal distribution
# X: is a given sample percentage
# P(X<=.085)
pnorm(.085, mu, sigma)
## [1] 0.3756493

Copy/paste/modify to explore the CLT prediction for the percentage of sample percentages above 9.2%.

# rel. frequency
1 - sum(samp_dist_pct > 0.092)/length(samp_dist_pct)
## [1] 0.5456
# CLT prediction
1 - pnorm(.092, mu, sigma)
## [1] 0.4894323

Lets visualize

# use bins = 10
compare_sampdist_norm(samp_dist_pct, mu, sigma, bins = 15)

CLT for means

Lets assume the actual population mean is \(\mu\), the population standard deviation is \(\sigma\), and we consider samples of size \(n\).

  • Assumptions: For most populations, if \(n>30\) then we can assume the Central Limit Theorem applies reasonably well.
  • Result: The sampling distribution of sample means (\(\bar x\)) can be modeled with a normal distribution where
    • the mean is \(\mu\), and
    • the standard deviation is \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).

Example. We already generated simulated sampling distribution of mean heights for our people data under the name samp_dist_means, assuming samples of size 50. Moreover, recall that the population of heights had the following parameters:

people %>% summarise(
    mean = mean(heights),
    sd = sd(heights)
)

Lets compare the sampling distribution against the CLT predictions!

# CLT parameters
# mu = ...
mu <- 65.577

# sigma = ...
sigma <- 3.463829/sqrt(50)

#On your own, try out a probability versus percentage comparison
# Compare the rel. frequency of mean height for a group of 50 is less than 65 inches
sum(samp_dist_means < 65)/length(samp_dist_means)
## [1] 0.12
# to the probability predicted by the CLT
pnorm(65, mu, sigma)
## [1] 0.1194204

Try comparing rel. frequencies against probability for sample means between 65 and 66 inches:

sum(samp_dist_means < 66)/length(samp_dist_means) - 
    sum(samp_dist_means < 65)/length(samp_dist_means)
## [1] 0.687
pnorm(66, mu,sigma) - pnorm(65, mu, sigma)
## [1] 0.6866519

Lets visualize:

compare_sampdist_norm(samp_dist_means,mu,sigma, bins = 20)

Central Limit Theorem Problems

In the examples we present below, we start laying the foundation for how we will use the Central Limit Theorem to do statistical inference. In addition to applying the Central Limit Theorem, we explore the logic and important ideas that underly our two main techniques.

An application with percentages (Hypothesis Testing)

Suppose that a friend of your claims that 50% of all Labrador Retrievers have a brown coat (chocolate labs). You are pretty sure that your friend is wrong, but how can you demonstrate that?

You decide to use a data driven approach, and so through the American Kennel Club, you get a random sample of 100 Labrador Retrievers: labradors.

  1. What percentage of your sample have brown coat?
# place code here
labradors %>%
    select(coat) %>% 
    table() %>% prop.table()
## coat
##  black  brown yellow 
##   0.48   0.23   0.29

Our sample percentage is 23%, \(\hat p = 0.23\).

IF I assume my friend is right, I get to assume the population percentage \(p = 0.5\).

What are my CLT params assuming my friend is right?

mu <- 0.5
sigma <- sqrt(0.5*(1-0.5)/100)
sigma
## [1] 0.05
  1. Assuming your friend is correct, what percentage of the sample would you have expected? Why?

Answer: I should be seeing sample percentages around 50%, if my friend is right. This is because the CLT tells us the sample percentages, on average, equal the population percentage of 50% (assuming, again, my friend is right).

  1. Does your sample summary seem likely if your friend is correct? Why or why not?

Answer: A sample percentage of 23% wouldnt happen very often according to the CLT; it should around 50% with some variation. In fact, our standard deviation of the sampling distribution (Standard Error) is about 5%, so anything outside of the 40% - 60% range is unusual due to the Empirical Rule.

  1. Assuming your friend is correct, what would the sampling distribution of the percentage of chocolate labs look like according to the central limit theorem (CLT)?

  2. Using the normal distribution from the previous exercise, compute the probability of getting a sample percentage like yours (answer to question 1), or something smaller?

pnorm(0.23, .5, sqrt(.5*(1-.25)/100) )
## [1] 5.190497e-06

(Well call these \(P\)-values soon)

  1. Is the probability from question 5 big or small? Explain how this provides evidence against your friends claim.

Answer: This probability is very small (.00000003). This provides evidence against the claim because if my friend is right, our sample should basically never appear! Its far too unlikely!

An application with means (Confidence Intervals)

Your sample of 100 Labrador retrievers also contains information on their weights, and we want to use that to make an educated guess about the mean weight for all Labradors (\(\mu\)).

labradors %>%
    summarize(
        meanWT = mean(weights),
        sdWT = sd(weights),
        sample_size = n()
    )
  1. According to the central limit theorem, what is the mean and standard deviation of the sampling distribution of mean weights? (Your answer should be in terms of the population parameters \(\mu\) and \(\sigma\), the mean and standard deviation of individual Labrador weights.)

mean = \(\mu\), the population mean weight for labs sd = \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), where \(\sigma\) is the population std. deviation.

  1. According to the central limit theorem, will the sampling distribution of sample mean weights be normally distributed?

Yes, because we have n = 100, which is much more than 30.

  1. Does the Empirical Rule apply to the sampling distribution of sample mean weights? If so, where should we expect 95% of potential sample means to fall?

It should fall within \(2\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) from the actual population mean; in the interval: \[(\mu - 2\sigma/\sqrt{n}, \mu + 2\sigma/\sqrt{n})\]

  1. Said differently, for this middle 95% of potential sample mean guesses, we will be at least how close to the actual population mean \(\mu\)?

95% of the time, Ill be within \(2\sigma/\sqrt{n}\) from the actual population mean Im trying to estimate!

  • Its likely that Im in the middle, although I dont know whether Im bigger or smaller than the actual population mean \(\mu\).

So I will guess an interval based on my sample mean, and widen by \(2\sigma/\sqrt{n}\).

  • Wait, I dont know \(\sigma\), so Ill use my sample standard deviation as the best I can do to replace it.

Guess:

71.9884 + c(-2,2)*9.030984/sqrt(100)
## [1] 70.1822 73.7946
  1. Would increasing our sample size lead to guesses that are closer or farther away from the population mean? Can you use the CLT to explain why?

Increasing the sample size leads to a smaller value for \(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\), according to the CLT. This is roughly a measure of how close our guesses are to the actual population parameter. This means larger sample sizes tend to lead to guesses that are close to the parameter we are trying to find.
